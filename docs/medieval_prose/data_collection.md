## Data Collection

The corpus is constructed from medieval texts that are **publicly available** in at least two different languages.

The primary criterion for inclusion is the existence of **parallel or comparable versions of the same text in at least two languages**, enabling cross-lingual alignment. When additional language versions are available, they are incorporated into the corpus to enrich the multilingual signal.

While the core configuration relies on **language A ↔ language B** alignment, the corpus may also include:

- additional languages (e.g., A ↔ B ↔ C),  
- or multiple witnesses within the same language (e.g., B₁, B₂),  

provided that a clear cross-lingual alignment can be established.

Importantly, the presence of multiple versions in the same language does not replace the requirement for cross-lingual pairing. These additional witnesses are used to **increase variation and robustness**, but the fundamental unit of the corpus remains **cross-lingual semantic alignment**.

This approach allows us to combine:

- a stable bilingual alignment backbone,  
- with richer multilingual and intra-lingual variation when available.



----

As this is already a highly time-consuming task, we currently prioritize multilingual texts whose translations do not exhibit major divergences in narrative structure, as these are more suitable for consistent and reliable semantic alignment.


All the texts and its sources used to build this corpus are detailed and available [here](corpus_sources.csv).
